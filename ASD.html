What to do after diagnosis

1. Acceptance and a new perspective
Your child is not “broken” or sick in the usual sense. Their brain works differently. Acceptance is not giving up — it is the decision to learn the “manual” for your specific child to help them adapt.

2. Comprehensive assessment
To create a clear plan, you need a solid foundation. Get evaluations from a team of specialists: a neurologist, psychiatrist, and speech therapist. This helps rule out related physical issues (for example, hearing problems or vitamin deficiencies).

3. Evidence-based therapy
Don’t waste time on “magic pills.” Focus on proven methods:
ABA therapy — teaching life skills through a reward system.
Denver Model (ESDM) — development through play for young children.
Sensory integration — helping manage sensory experiences.

4. Parents’ energy is the foundation
ASD therapy is a marathon, not a sprint. If a parent is exhausted, the child’s progress slows down. Allow yourself to rest, attend support groups, and remember: a happy parent is the best therapist.

5. Stability and routine
Predictability reduces anxiety. Use visual schedules (PECS cards) so the child understands what comes next. A clear daily rhythm creates a safe space where learning becomes easier.

Methods

Method: Matching game
Goal: develop language understanding and choice.
1. Use 2 cards
2. Ask: “Where is the apple?”
3. Guide by hand
4. Child chooses independently
5. Increase difficulty
Main rule: praise

Method: Token system
Goal: teach task completion.
1. Show the reward (cartoon)
2. Create 3 slots
3. Give tokens
4. Fill them — reward
Main rule: give tokens quickly at first

Method: “First — Then”
Goal: reduce anxiety.
1. Use cards
2. “First…, then…”
3. Show visually
4. Immediate reward
Main rule: instant reinforcement
